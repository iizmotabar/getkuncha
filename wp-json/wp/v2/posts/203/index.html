When you have a website, your ultimate aim is always to attract the perfect audience and retain their attention. But how would you know if your viewership is actually valuable to your business or not? \u00a0One metric that is very useful in knowing if your audience is interested in your business or not, is \u201cBounce Rate\u201d.<\/p>\n

Both high and low bounce rates are not favorable for your business. There are many factors that are involved in keeping it stable. To maintain a good bounce rate, you should be able to use the right tools to monitor and, if need be, even reduce it.<\/p>\n

In this article, you will learn everything about keeping a good bounce rate to grow and achieve your business goals.<\/p>\n

Choosing a traffic analysis tool for your website<\/strong><\/h2>\n

The first step after launching your website is to integrate a traffic analysis tool. Most CMS platforms like WordPress, Squarespace, ..etc offer dashboards for your website traffic, but those aren\u2019t very useful after all.<\/p>\n

You will have to integrate a powerful analytics tool that you can rely on, one that can help you analyze your website traffic. Our favorite analytics tool is Google Analytics, which offers a wide range of reports, is easy to integrate and displays a complete picture of your website traffic.<\/p>\n

There are other analytics tools as well but for the sake of this article, I will stick with Google Analytics since it\u2019s a top analytics tool<\/a> and is used by millions of websites.<\/p>\n

If you don\u2019t have Google Analytics already installed on your website, I highly recommend you to follow our step by step guide. <\/a><\/p>\n

Checking Bounce Rate in Google Analytics<\/strong><\/h2>\n

Once you have analytics installed on your website, you can easily view the major metrics like Bounce Rate, Users, Sessions, Avg. session Duration..etc. The home page reports in Google Analytics displays very important metrics for your website.<\/p>\n

On the very top of the page, you can see your bounce rate. The data that appears on the homepage is set to last 7 days by default. But you can change the date range by navigating to each particular report.<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

You can view the bounce rate in multiple reports. For example, you can see the bounce rate for each traffic channel or even traffic campaign separately. If you navigate to Acquisition >> All Traffic >> Channels<\/strong>, you can see the bounce rate for the traffic coming from each channel.<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

This will help you get a better view of what channel, the most relevant traffic is coming from. You can then align your marketing strategy for each traffic channel, accordingly.<\/p>\n

You can even view bounce rates for each page on your website. To do this, navigate to Behavior >> Site Content >> All Pages<\/strong>.<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

Now you know how to view bounce rates in Google Analytics, it\u2019s time to actually understand what bounce rate is.<\/p>\n

What is Bounce Rate in Google Analytics?<\/strong><\/h2>\n

When a user lands on your website and leaves without visiting any other pages, the user is considered to be a bounced user. The bounce rate, in turn, gives you the percentage of site visitors who exit from the landing page without checking out any other page of the website.<\/p>\n

\n

What does it mean to have a \u2018high\u2019 or a \u2018low\u2019 bounce rate?<\/strong><\/h2>\n

When a website has a high bounce rate, chances are that the people coming to the website aren\u2019t finding things of interest to them and therefore, are leaving without engaging with the website further. If a website has a low bounce rate, chances are that the users find the content to be relevant and useful, and so they decide to explore further by going to various other pages.<\/p>\n

Source: Digital Marketing and Analytics<\/p>\n

<\/h2>\n

When is a high bounce rate not necessarily bad<\/strong><\/h2>\n

The bounce rate of your website can change if you start considering and set various actions on your landing page as \u201cevents\u201d. For instance, it could be a video play, a page scroll beyond a certain point etc.<\/p>\n

When a user comes to your website and leaves without going to any other page BUT does play the video or scrolls through the landing page beyond the set point, the user will not be considered a bounced user, because, he did, in fact, register an interaction with the website.<\/p>\n

When the interactions of your interest aren\u2019t marked as \u201cevents\u201d, chances are all users who come to the landing page of your website and leave even after a playing a video for example, will be considered to have bounced off, and thus your bounce rate will be a lot higher than it should be.<\/p>\n

In this scenario, the best way to handle bounce rate is by sending an event interaction to Google Analytics on events that are important to you, such as, a page scroll or a video interaction.<\/p>\n

Reducing bounce rate involves the collective effort of people managing the site, it’s marketing, and analytics. Bounce rate, at end of day, is a quick indicator of traffic that comes to the site and the experience they have on the first page of your site.<\/p>\n

Below is a list of 17 ways you can follow to reduce bounce rate.<\/p>\n

1. Use Analytics for Your Advantage<\/strong><\/h3>\n

As mentioned earlier, setting up an analytics tool like Google Analytics will help you measure your website traffic and monitor the correct bounce rate.<\/p>\n

Setting up Google Analytics is easy, so make sure you add the tracking code throughout all the pages in the right place. Setting up analytics incorrectly results in a sudden drop of bounce rate<\/a>.<\/p>\n

The next important thing is to configure your analytics account<\/a> to make sure the data you capture, is accurate. This process mainly includes filtering internal and spam traffic.<\/p>\n

If you have a single landing page or your site\u2019s bounce rate is high, set up event tracking to measure user actions. This will help you measure if the users are interacting with your site content before they bounce off. The event tracking data will ultimately help you decide if you need to change the content or CTA\u2019s on the page.<\/p>\n

Setting up event tracking is fairly simple via Google Tag Manager. You can track button clicks<\/a> on your site and even scroll depth<\/a> if it\u2019s a single page site.<\/p>\n

2. Set your website goals <\/strong><\/h3>\n

Whenever you set up a website, you set it up for a reason. It is either for lead generation or for selling products online or for another purpose. After setting up analytics on your website, the next important thing is to set up goals.<\/p>\n

Goals in Google Analytics help you measure the conversions on your websites. If you have a lead generation website, setting up a goal for the form submissions will help you track the number of visitors submitting the form out of all the website visitors. This in turn, calculates the conversion rate for your landing page or website.<\/p>\n

To set up goals in Google Analytics, follow our in-depth guide here<\/a>.<\/p>\n

Another important reason to set up goals is to see how the bounce rates and conversion rates relate. If you have a high bounce rate, but still the conversion rate is good, you may not need to change anything on your website.<\/p>\n

3. Focus more on high performing marketing channels\/campaigns<\/strong><\/h3>\n

The internet is full of social media platforms, search engines (Google, Bing) and online communities. The most common marketing tactic, then, is to find places where your audience likes to hang out and promote your content there.<\/p>\n

In Google Analytics we can easily see where the traffic is coming from. If you don\u2019t know how to open your analytics account, navigate to Acquisition >> All Traffic >> Channels<\/strong>.<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

You can see a list of all traffic channels in the image above. Just look at the number of users from each channel, avg. session duration, bounce rate,\u00a0and ecommerce conversion rate.<\/p>\n

You can see that affiliates and direct traffic have a high conversion rate. The bounce rate and avg session duration are good. The result is that you continue the same marketing strategies for these two channels and focus more on channels with low conversion rates.<\/p>\n

You can even stop your marketing strategies for channels with a high bounce rate and very low conversion rates as you can see for social and display channels.<\/p>\n

To see the overview of your site speed metrics, navigate to Behavior >> Site Speed >> Overview<\/strong>.<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

6. Consider A\/B testing for your new landing pages<\/strong><\/h3>\n

A\/B testing helps you develop high converting landing pages for your business. A\/B testing means creating multiple versions of your landing page and dividing the traffic on both pages to see which one performs better.<\/p>\n

The one that performs better will be your final landing page. There are other types of tests that help you test multiple CTA\u2019s or even headlines on the same page.<\/p>\n

7. Migrate to Google Tag Manager<\/strong><\/h3>\n

You might be wondering why I am focusing more on installing Google Tag Manager. To understand the need of GTM, you have to first understand at how GA calculates bounce rate.<\/p>\n

To better understand this, let\u2019s create a scenario. In the first case, you have a single page landing page while the second page consists of multiple pages.<\/p>\n

Single page bounce rate:<\/strong><\/p>\n

If you have just analytics installed on your page, even a visitor who lands on your page reads all the content and left will be considered as a high bounce rate. For example, if there were 100 visitors on your website, out of the 90 left on the same page, the bounce rate will be calculated as 90\/100*100 = 90%<\/p>\n

All website bounce rate:<\/strong><\/p>\n

In this case, the visitors are navigating to other pages on your website which shows interaction with the site and hence reduces the bounce rate. For example, if there were 90 people entered your site last day, 20 bounced on the same page, bounce rate will be: 20\/90*100= 22%.<\/p>\n

So here comes the major question. How how to control the bounce rate even if the visitor read all the content on the single page and bounced.<\/p>\n

This is where we need Google Tag Manager to track events. Events will send interaction hits which will control the bounce rate.<\/p>\n

You can set up event tracking on a button click, link click or even a page scroll. Follow our step by step guide to track events with Google Tag Manager<\/a>.<\/p>\n

11. Make The Website User-Friendly<\/strong><\/h3>\n

Make sure that the design of your website ensures that your visitors have as seamless an experience as possible. In order to stay on your site for longer, they should find it easy to navigate and find things they want to read, quickly and efficiently.<\/p>\n

The same goes for any forms you may have on your site. Make sure that are viewable, accessible and easy to fill out.<\/p>\n

12. Curate Fresh & High-Quality Content<\/strong><\/h3>\n

It goes without saying that your audience is always on the lookout, for fresh new solutions to their problems. It is then, highly advisable to keep your site up-to-date with high-quality content that your audience finds most useful.<\/p>\n

13. Use Clear call to Actions<\/strong><\/h3>\n

14. Avoid unnecessary pop-ups<\/strong><\/h3>\n

Pop-ups are good for promoting your services and newsletter subscriptions, and that\u2019s all great, but they should be used in a way that it doesn\u2019t distract the user. It\u2019s better to trigger a pop-up on an exit intent instead of when a user is really focusing on your content.<\/p>\n

15. Include Testimonials & Social Proof<\/strong><\/h3>\n

When you are selling online services, it\u2019s important that you provide client testimonials and social proof, in order to build trust. This helps in capturing the visitor attention. Display your potential leads, top reviews and anything out of the box you have done.<\/p>\n

16. Remove Irrelevant Ads<\/strong><\/h3>\n

Make sure your website is not crowded with irrelevant or too many ads. Display ads that are relevant to the content and the visitor.<\/p>\n
