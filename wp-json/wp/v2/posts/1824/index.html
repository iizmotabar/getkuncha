One of the primary reasons for inaccurate data in Google Analytics is spam traffic. This segment of traffic isn\u2019t real and is a combination of bots and spiders crawling your website. The unwanted traffic that comes to your website without any of your investment or efforts is known as spam traffic in Google Analytics.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

We always want to see accurate data in Google Analytics, so the first thing we do while configuring Google Analytics<\/a> is to enable the bot filtering option under view settings.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

This isn\u2019t enough to get rid of unwanted traffic, you will be seeing new spam sources time to time in your account. So the best practice is to have a Google Analytics audit<\/a> twice or thrice a year.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Recently I was analyzing data in a Google Analytics account and found that direct traffic was high and unusual. After drilling down, I found the traffic was coming from a spam source.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

In this tutorial, I will teach you how to find spam traffic and how to create an advanced filter to exclude that particular traffic in any view.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

1. Finding the Spam Traffic in Google Analytics<\/strong><\/h2>\n\n\n\n

There are three primary metrics to verify when checking for spam traffic.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

  1. 100% new users<\/li>
  2. 100% or 0% bounce rate<\/li>
  3. pages\/session equals 1<\/li><\/ol>\n\n\n\n

    When you see all the above things true, you can consider that traffic to be spam.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    Let\u2019s take this step by step<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    Go to default channel grouping report, and click the channel you think is showing unusual traffic. In my case, the suspicious channel is direct.<\/p>\n\n\n
